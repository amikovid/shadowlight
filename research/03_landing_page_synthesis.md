## 1. Max Hertan's House Style (what he already approves of)

Max's voice across his own companies is remarkably consistent. If we want him to look at our page and feel "this person gets me," we mirror these patterns.

### Snowball (current, his most-public brand)

**Tone characteristics:**
- Bold statements with no hedging: *"We don't follow YouTube best practices. We create them."*
- Specificity through numbers: 90B views, 110M subscribers, $300M revenue driven
- Confident bordering on cocky, but always backed by proof
- Casual section labels in lowercase: *"how we roll", "meet ryan hashemi"*
- Long-form storytelling on About sections (Ryan's bio reads like a personal essay, not a corporate mission statement)
- Strong sign-offs: *"When you're ready to grow, we're ready to go."* / *"Let's get rolling."*
- Names the unfair advantage explicitly. Doesn't whisper credentials. Says *"It's taken $20M and 15 years on YouTube to learn all I know."*

**Structural patterns:**
- Hero with single bold promise, no feature list
- Massive marquee of social proof (90B VIEWS, 110M SUBSCRIBERS) as a section
- Process explained in 3-4 verbs (Train · Ideate · Create · Scale)
- Single big CTA repeated several times down the page
- Minimal nav. They don't want you wandering off.

### Megaphone (his exited company)

- Built around the "leverage" idea — Max ran it 2-3 days/week
- Specialties listed as outcomes, not services (paid ads, CRO, social — all framed by what they produced, $1B+ in revenue for clients)
- "Australia's fastest-growing and most-awarded" — superlatives, not adjectives

### Silvi (consumer brand, closest analog to Volley)

- Origin-story-driven: "Two friends had teenage acne, built a pillowcase that kills 99.97% of bacteria"
- Specific science claims: silver ions, Mulberry silk, 4.5x more breathable than cotton
- Positions itself against an entire category: *"a market saturated in harsh topicals and prescription medicine"*
- Max's own quote: *"A great product should do a lot of the marketing itself. Customers should be selling it to their network."* — this is the philosophy. The page should feel inevitable, not pushy.

### Max's personal LinkedIn voice

- Numbered lists of lessons learned (he loves "6 biggest lessons")
- Counter-positioning explicitly named as a strategy: *"Length: business podcasts were 60+ mins, so we'll make ours <12. Visuals: editing was stale, so we'll make tactical visuals people could screenshot. Setting: most interviews were on podcast sets, so we'll do them in the founder's living room."*
- This is a giant clue. **Max thinks in counter-positioning.** Volley's landing page should counter-position against Speak/Duolingo/etc. explicitly.

### What we steal from Max

| From | What we take |
|---|---|
| Snowball | Confidence, big numbers as section anchors, lowercase eyebrows, single repeated CTA |
| Megaphone | Outcome-as-headline, leverage thinking |
| Silvi | Origin/founder credibility, science-backed claims, category counter-positioning |
| Max's LinkedIn | Explicit counter-positioning, numbered structure, "6 lessons" cadence |

## 2. Language App Category — What's Working Right Now

### Speak (the dominant Volley competitor)

**Their hero copy (verbatim):**
> *"Talk out loud, get instant feedback, and become fluent with the world's most advanced AI language tutor."*
> CTA: *Start Speaking →*

**Tagline lower:** *"Practice Daily. Speak Fluently."*

**Key positioning moves:**
- "Speaking out loud" is THE wedge they've claimed
- Speak Tutor framed as a character ("on-the-go conversational partner", "keeps you motivated and accountable")
- Testimonial leans into a competitive frame: *"I took Spanish classes for seven years, used Duolingo, used Pimsleur... I still struggled to speak. This app is like having a personal tutor without the anxiety"*
- They've already taken the obvious "speak vs learn" angle. **We need a different wedge.**

### MakesYouFluent (smaller competitor, paid landing page)

- Hero: *"Start speaking a new language from day one"*
- Trust signal: 28-day "speak confidently" promise
- Heavy on roleplay framing (job interview, weather, debates)
- 10-day refund window — not as bold as our 30-day promise

### Langua (currently rated #1 in roundup articles per the audit)

- Wedge: *"voices cloned from native speakers, conversations feel genuinely human"*
- Trust through honesty: their own marketing admits "the AI might miss a correction"
- Suggested replies for beginners — removes the friction of "I don't know what to say"

### Duolingo (the giant we're dunking on)

- Gamified, streak-driven, vocabulary-first
- Positioned for casual learners, not aspirational fluent speakers
- $13/month — the price anchor everyone thinks of first

### Category conventions (and which to break)

| Convention | What everyone does | What we should do |
|---|---|---|
| Hero promise | "Become fluent" / "Start speaking" | Pick a fight, don't make a generic promise |
| Visual proof | App screenshots | **Live conversation demo** (the audit calls this out: raw screen capture beats produced content) |
| Social proof | App Store reviews | Specific named user with specific outcome |
| Feature explanation | 3-icon grid | Numbered counter-positioning ("3 things every other app gets wrong") |
| CTA | "Start free trial" | Actionable verb tied to the wedge ("Start talking") |
| Pricing | Hidden until pricing page | Visible in hero — $99 isn't a secret to be ashamed of |

**The wedge nobody has claimed yet:** *the conversation you've been avoiding.* Speak owns "speaking." Langua owns "realistic AI." Duolingo owns "habit." Nobody owns the emotional truth that adults learning a language are *terrified to actually use it with humans*. That's our opening.

## 3. What 2026 Landing Page Data Actually Shows

Source: Digital Applied, *Landing Page Conversion: 2,000 Pages Tested in 2026* — A/B-tested 2,000 pages across B2B SaaS, agencies, DTC ecommerce, lead-gen, and webinar funnels between October 2025 and March 2026, every result cleared 95% significance with at least 1,000 sessions per variant. URL: https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/landing-page-conversion-study-2000-pages-tested-2026

The findings that re-shape our page:

**Single-stat hero +18% lift, video hero -7%, "no hero" +4% (B2B SaaS only)**
- Quote: *"Single-stat hero — One big number as the headline (e.g., '127× faster') +18% highest lift."*
- Translation: a hero with one giant number beats stock images and beats autoplay video. Volley's available number is *"1,000+ learners practicing this week,"* small but honest. The split visual (AI mock + tutor card) is our compromise — proof asset rather than proof number.

**Named-customer specificity +22% lift; press-logo strips +5% (weakest pattern)**
- Quote: *"Named-customer count — '8 of the Fortune 50' +22% lift · highest in category"* and *"Press logos — 'As featured in TechCrunch, Forbes' +5% lift · weakest pattern."*
- We don't have Fortune 50 names; we have Sarah K. / Anders L. / Marcus T. with specific outcomes. The named-specific pattern is what we're cloning, even at smaller scale.

**Sticky-bottom CTAs +11%, above-fold CTA +6%, BOTH = +12% (no compounding)**
- Quote: *"Sticky-bottom + above-fold CTA — Both patterns combined +12% no compounding."*
- The sticky absorbs almost all the benefit. We add a sticky-bottom CTA on scroll. We do NOT need a second above-fold CTA once the sticky exists. (Note: our current build keeps a secondary above-fold CTA "See the loop" — this is a deliberate trade-off documented in [JUSTIFICATION_DOC.md](../JUSTIFICATION_DOC.md) §B.1, not an oversight.)

**AI copy loses -2% on DTC, -5% on webinar pages**
- Quote: *"DTC ecommerce -2% / Webinar -5%."* B2B SaaS and B2B services see small positive lift from AI copy; consumer pages don't.
- Volley is DTC. Our copy must feel hand-written. Specific, opinionated, slightly informal.

**Em-dash threshold: more than 2 em-dashes per 100 words → -5% on DTC**
- Quote: *"Pages with more than 2 em-dashes per 100 words lose 5% on average."*
- Our zero-em-dash rule is conservative; the data threshold is roughly 2-per-100. We hold zero anyway because the visual signature alone is the AI-tell, regardless of density.

**Banned-word penalty: -8%**
- Quote: *"Pages using `delve`, `leverage`, `synergize`, or `optimize your experience` lose 8%."*
- This is the largest single-pattern penalty in the study. Our banned-word list extends beyond the study's four (we also block "comprehensive, seamless, foster, facilitate, elevate, robust, holistic") because they trigger the same pattern recognition in 2026 readers. The study quantifies the floor; the cultural list extends the ceiling.

**Hero rules of thumb that still hold:**
- Headline under 44 characters when possible (ours can break this if punchy enough)
- One primary CTA above the fold (single, not multi)
- 5-second test: visitor must understand *what, who, why* in 5 seconds

### Proven copy frameworks we can pull from

**The What-Why-Who hero formula (LandingRabbit):**
- *What* the product does
- *Why* it matters / what's in it for the reader
- *Who* it's for
- Volley applied: *What* (AI conversation partner) · *Why* (so you can finally actually talk) · *Who* (adults who've tried 5 apps and still can't order coffee in the language)

**The 4C framework (Flow Agency):**
- Clear, Concise, Compelling, Credible — every section answers all four

**Pain → Solution → Proof structure (most B2C SaaS):**
- Hero: name the pain
- Below fold: show the solution working
- Then: stack proof until objections die
- Volley applied: Hero (you've avoided this for years) → Demo (you can actually do it) → Comparison/Why (here's why this works) → Guarantee (we'll bet our money on it)

## What this means for our page

Putting all three buckets together, the framework that emerges:

1. **Hero like Snowball, copy like Silvi, wedge nobody else owns**
   Open with a counter-positioning statement (Snowball move) backed by a specific, scientifically-honest promise (Silvi move) that claims the unclaimed emotional territory: *the fear of actually speaking.*

2. **The animated demo is the hero asset, not a section**
   Single-stat heroes win. Video autoplay loses. An animated chat is closer to a single-stat hero — it's a lightweight, looping, instantly-readable proof of concept. It IS the hook.

3. **Counter-positioning section instead of a feature grid**
   Three numbered "things every other app gets wrong" beats three icons with feature names. This is Max's natural cadence and it's also a proven copy framework.

4. **Specific named user, specific outcome, no logo strip**
   We don't have a logo strip. We don't need one. One Marcus T. with the cried-then-corrected-grammar story is worth more than 50 trust badges.

5. **Sticky-bottom CTA, single hero CTA, no above-fold-and-sticky redundancy**
   "Start talking" repeated in hero and final CTA. Sticky on scroll. That's the full CTA strategy.

6. **30-day money-back guarantee as a category-defining trust signal**
   Nobody else does this. Putting it in writing forces honesty (the product has to actually work) and removes the highest-friction objection at the $99 price point.

7. **Voice rule: no AI-detection tells**
   No em-dashes. No "leverage." No "comprehensive." No "seamless." No "navigate." Hand-written feeling, opinionated, specific.

## Open questions for the positioning lock

1. Is the wedge **"the conversation you've been avoiding for years"** (emotional, mature) or **"every other app made you fluent in tapping, not talking"** (more aggressive counter-positioning)? Or a third frame?
2. How much of Volley's specific tech (real-time voice, stories, personalized learning) do we name vs. fold into the experience description?
3. Do we want a "founder's note" section (Silvi-style origin story) or skip it because the founder isn't yet a public figure?
4. Pricing visible in hero, or below the fold with the guarantee?

Answer those four and we have everything we need to write final copy.
