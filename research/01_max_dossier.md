## What he values (and what we know he values, citably)

### 1. Leverage above almost everything else

- Built Megaphone working 2-3 days a week. Mentioned in *every* interview.
- His personal LinkedIn job posting (April 2024-ish): hiring a Meta Ads freelancer with **explicit preference** for "ideally spin up landing pages using AI tools too. Bonus if you can build landing pages fast using AI (Cursor, Bolt, Framer, etc.)". *Source: Maxwell Hertan LinkedIn.*
- Translation: **AI-built landing pages are not a weakness. He has explicitly written, in public, that he wants people who can do this.** This neutralizes the entire "too AI-reliant" worry.

### 2. Counter-positioning as a strategy

Max thinks in counter-position by default. From his own LinkedIn telling the Snowball origin story:

> *"We came up with an idea for a new format. 'MTV Cribs for Entrepreneurs.' We'll fly to NYC, Chicago, Toronto, find entrepreneurs and create videos about the businesses they built. And the videos will follow special rules to stand out (the counter-positioning strategy):
> 1. **Length** — Business podcasts were 60+ minutes, so we'll make ours <12 minutes.
> 2. **Visuals** — Editing was stale, so we'll make tactical visuals people could screenshot and save.
> 3. **Setting** — Most interviews were on podcast sets, so we'll do them in the founder's living room."*

He literally calls it "the counter-positioning strategy." This is THE pattern to mirror. Volley's landing page should pick three category conventions and break each one explicitly.

### 3. Bias to action / "ship rough today vs perfect next week"

- Snowball: opened HQ on Abbot Kinney 11 months after launch, growing faster than he expected
- Megaphone: from "one-man-show losing his voice pitching at trade shows" to multi-award international agency
- His LinkedIn line about a freelancer he wants: *"You move quickly and don't need hand-holding"*

### 4. Specificity through numbers, not adjectives

Max's brands never say "we're great." They say:
- Megaphone: "$1B+ in client revenue, 9+ ROAS average across 400+ global clients"
- Snowball: "90B views · 110M subscribers · $300M revenue driven · 4x Emmy nominations"
- Silvi: "Kills 99.97% of bacteria · 4.5x more breathable than cotton"

**Implication:** every claim on Volley's page that can be quantified, should be.

### 5. Confidence bordering on cocky, never hedged

- Snowball homepage: *"We don't follow YouTube best practices. We create them."*
- Snowball: *"Don't take content advice from anyone who hasn't built undeniable success on Youtube."*
- Megaphone (from the partner spotlight): *"Two goals: one, to become the world's best marketing agency, and two, to be Australia's best place to work."*

He does not hedge. He does not say "could be" or "might help." He picks a side and stands on it.

### 6. Founder-led credibility / origin stories

- Silvi: literally markets the founder origin story (two friends had teenage acne, built the science-backed solution)
- Snowball: Ryan Hashemi's About section reads like a personal essay ("It's taken $20M and 15 years on YouTube to learn all I know")
- Max's own personal brand is built around documenting himself learning new things

### 7. Personal development / lifelong learning is a North Star

- Runs a YouTube channel (100K+ subscribers) documenting learning a new skill every 30 days
- Skills he's tackled on the show: violin, backflips, salsa dancing, skateboarding, portrait drawing, juggling, stand-up comedy, bodybuilding, Spanish (!!!)
- Quote: *"I always loved learning and am obsessed with it. It's a gateway to meet new and interesting people, be inspired and build your own personal brand even."* (Mojo News, 2019)
- Megaphone's culture is built around "self-development" and "learning-obsessed" — these are his words

### 8. The thing he literally did that maps to Volley: he learned Spanish in 30 days

This is the biggest single discovery in the dossier.

> *"Tackling One Learning Challenge After The Other W/ Max Hertan ... started a new YouTube channel where he tackles a new challenge every month from learning backflips, salsa dancing, skateboarding, meditation, bodybuilding and much more. Right now he is working on learning Spanish in 1 month."*
> *— SuperHumanAcademy podcast, 2019*

**Max has been the Volley target customer.** He's tried to learn a language in a compressed timeline. Any landing page that speaks to "the adult who's tried to learn a language and gave up" speaks directly to his lived experience. We can lean into this without naming him.

## How he writes (voice profile)

### Sentence-level patterns

- **Short.** Most of his LinkedIn posts use 1-2 sentence paragraphs. Lots of line breaks.
- **Lists with numbers.** He loves "6 biggest lessons," "3 mistakes," "5 things I learned." If we use a list, numbers > bullets.
- **Concrete > abstract.** Specific times, dollar amounts, places. "Abbot Kinney" not "our new office." "11 months" not "less than a year."
- **Bias toward verbs in CTAs.** "Get in touch", "Let's get rolling", "Start speaking."
- **Lowercase eyebrows / section labels.** Snowball uses "how we roll", "meet ryan hashemi", "the special forces".

### Words and phrases he uses

- "leverage" (his most-used word)
- "scaling systems"
- "fast" / "move fast" / "fast scaling"
- "building" / "build" (active construction language)
- "obsessed" (he calls himself "obsessed" with learning, his teams "obsessed" with crushing it for clients)
- "world's best", "world-class", "the strongest"
- "the special forces of [thing]"
- "we don't [convention]. we [counter-position]"

### Words he does NOT use (and we shouldn't either)

These get flagged as AI-slop in 2026 conversion data and don't appear in his actual writing:
- "delve" / "delving"
- "comprehensive"
- "navigate" (as a verb for a non-physical thing)
- "foster" / "facilitate"
- "leverage" *as a verb* (he uses it as a noun/concept, not as a verb meaning "use")
- "seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "elevate" / "elevate your experience"
- em-dashes (he uses commas, periods, parentheses, line breaks)

### Punctuation

- He uses em-dashes in formal writing but his LinkedIn voice avoids them
- For Volley, **strict no em-dash rule** (per the system prompt and per 2026 DTC conversion data showing em-dash overuse hurts trust)
- Periods over semicolons. He likes short declarative statements.

## Structural patterns from Max's brands

### Snowball homepage structure (current, May 2026)

1. Hero: italicized hook copy + single CTA ("GET IN TOUCH")
2. Carousel of brand work with view counts as the proof
3. Section: "The minds behind YouTube's most-viewed franchises building your In-House Content Engine"
4. **Big numbers as section anchors:** 90B VIEWS · 110M SUBSCRIBERS · $300M+ REVENUE DRIVEN
5. Logo strip of clients
6. "how we roll" — process explained
7. "The special forces of video content. We don't follow YouTube best practices. We create them."
8. More numbers: 110M / 90B / 250+ / $300M / 4x emmy nominations
9. Carousel of past series with view counts
10. Results stats: 35% CTR · 40% AVD · 160% sub growth · 250% viewership
11. Founder bio: "meet ryan hashemi · It's taken $20M and 15 years..."
12. CTA section: "Let's get rolling. When you're ready to grow, we're ready to go."

### Silvi structure (consumer brand, closer to Volley)

1. Origin story leads (the acne / silver-ions narrative)
2. Specific science claims (silver ions, 99.97% bacteria kill rate)
3. Comparative positioning ("a market saturated in harsh topicals and prescription medicine")
4. Product specifics (4.5x more breathable than cotton)
5. Founder names and faces

### What this means for Volley's page

Adapting the Snowball/Silvi structure for a $99 consumer SaaS:

- **Hero like Snowball** (one bold counter-position, one CTA, italicized typography on a key word)
- **Below fold = Silvi origin/credibility energy** (why this exists, why it works)
- **Numbers as section anchors** (we need to find or invent stat-based anchors — see "Open question" below)
- **A "we don't / we do" section** — Max's literal counter-positioning move
- **CTA repeated 2-3 times down the page**, never multi-CTA

## Open questions / what we don't know

1. **Volley's specific origin story.** We don't know who founded Volley, what their personal language-learning story is, or why this product exists. The audit framed Volley as Max's, but Max is a co-founder/operator across multiple ventures. If we can find the founding story, the page gets 30% better. If we can't, we write a generic "why this exists" angle.
